What the data says

  • Do the models beat a dumb baseline? Slightly. The best model's typical miss (4.4 units) is 43% better than just averaging recent sales (7.7). Real, but not dramatic — which is exactly what ~5 months of history buys you.
  • The Python SARIMA model is unstable on short data. 2 forecasts ran away to absurd numbers — the worst predicted 52358 units for SKU 11346 that actually sold 39. SARIMA's trend+seasonal differencing extrapolates wildly when it has only a few weeks to fit. These rare blow-ups wreck the average error, which is why this report leads with the median (typical) miss instead. Croston/TSB (the slow-mover model, ~2/3 of the catalog) was stable throughout.
  • Production model vs Python models? The Python models (typical miss 4.4) edge the production model (7.8) on typical accuracy, but only once you ignore their blow-ups. Not switch-ready until SARIMA is guarded.
  • Does XGBoost help? No. On the only honest test — Apr→May, the one month it never trained on — it did not improve the typical miss (2.9 → 12.0). Ignore its Jan/Feb/Mar numbers (marked ★): the model trained on those months, so it just memorised them — that's why they look spectacular and the pooled figure is misleading. With only 4 monthly snapshots to learn from, XGBoost has nothing real to correct yet.
  • Is there enough data? No — and this is the headline. History runs only Dec 2025 → Jun 2026, and May/June are thin (a sync gap). The early months (Jan→Feb) are weakest precisely because the models had ~6 weeks to learn from. The single highest-leverage fix is a full year of sales via the read_all_orders backfill — it lifts every model and is what SARIMA needs to stop misbehaving.

Recommendations

  1. For now, trust the forecast as a guide, not gospel: it's roughly baseline-good for steady & slow movers, but pair it with the owner's judgement on new and spiky SKUs.
  2. Get the full year of history (read_all_orders backfill). Every model above is data-starved; this is the one change that helps all of them at once.
  3. Guard SARIMA before ever putting the Python models live — cap any forecast at, say, 3× the product's trailing best month so a divergence can't propose a 100,000-unit reorder.
  4. Shelve XGBoost until a year of data exists — on 4 monthly snapshots it overfits and adds no real accuracy.
  5. Re-run this exact backtest each month as data accrues — it's now a one-command health check on the forecasting brain (npx tsx scripts/walkforward-backtest.ts).
